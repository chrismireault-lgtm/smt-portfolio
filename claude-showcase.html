Best Places to Shop (in order of value)

  • SidelineSwap — The #1 resale market for baseball and softball gear. Easton Ghost and Louisville Meta bats regularly show up 30–50% below retail. Filter by "like new" condition and sort by recently listed. Fast-moving inventory.
  • Facebook Marketplace — Search locally first. Parents selling after a season or size change is free money. Look for listings that say "only used one season" — usually honest. Lowball by 15%, they expect it.
  • eBay — Best for hunting a specific model. Always check completed sales (not current listings) to see what bats actually sold for, not just the ask price. This is the move most people skip.
  • Play It Again Sports — Physical store, you can tap the barrel before buying. Especially good for youth bats. Staff usually knows their inventory well.
  • Dick's Clearance (Oct–Nov) — End of season, they blow out prior-year models at 40–60% off. Functionally identical to current year. Set a reminder.

Red Flags When Buying Used

  • Cracks near the barrel or end cap — walk away immediately.
  • Dead spots — tap the barrel firmly, top to bottom. It should sound consistent. A dull thud in one spot means the bat is dead inside.
  • Loose end cap — grab and twist it. Any rotation is a problem.
  • Missing certification stamp — USA, USSSA, or BBCOR must match your league rules. Check the taper near the handle.
Pro move: When you find a bat you like on SidelineSwap, search that exact model on eBay completed sales first. If the asking price is at or below the eBay average, buy immediately. If it's above, send an offer. Most sellers on SidelineSwap are parents trying to recoup — they'll take 10–15% less without much pushback.

Crypto & Markets

The group chat asks. Claude thinks out loud.

Not financial advice — reasoning. There's a difference, and that difference is everything.

You

"I've been holding Bitcoin since 2021 and I'm still not back to my original entry. Should I be worried? Do I hold or do something different?"

Claude

The honest answer depends on one thing: why you bought in 2021 — and whether that reason has actually changed. Most people conflate "I'm not profitable yet" with "I was wrong." Those are very different problems.

If your thesis was long-term

Bitcoin as a global store of value over 10–20 years — nothing material has changed. FTX, the ETF approvals, the 2024 halving — these all fit within the expected volatility of an asset still finding its price. You're inside a normal cycle. The 2021 peak was euphoria. This is consolidation.

If your thesis was short-term

Then the honest confrontation is timeline mismatch, not crypto itself. Bitcoin rarely rewards a 1–2 year horizon. If you bought expecting to sell higher within a year or two, the exit question becomes: has enough time passed to change the bet size or time frame?

What's Actually Different Since 2021

  • Spot ETFs are live (Jan 2024) — Institutional buying through ETFs changed the demand structure. Less retail-driven volatility, more slow accumulation from large balance sheets. That's structurally bullish over time.
  • The 2024 halving happened — Supply cut in half. Historically, 12–18 month bull cycles follow. We're inside that window now.
  • Macro is shifting — Bitcoin correlates with risk assets when rates are high and liquidity is tight. When central banks ease, it decouples. Watch M2 money supply trends, not just BTC price charts.
The one thing most long-term holders get wrong: They sell at the first sign of fear after surviving the worst of the drawdown. If your position is sized right — meaning you'd be okay if it went to zero — then the only real question is whether your thesis is intact. For most 2021 buyers with a long view, it is.

Size matters more than timing here. The right amount is whatever lets you hold with indifference — not desperation, not hope. That's the actual edge.

Sports & Parenting

The questions you're actually asking about your kids.

Not generic advice from a parenting blog. Actual reasoning informed by sports science, development research, and real baseball culture.

You

"My 12-year-old is decent at both hitting and pitching. His coach wants him to specialize in pitching and drop hitting. Should I listen to him?"

Claude

This is one of the genuinely important decisions in youth baseball and the research is clear enough that I'll lead with it: early specialization has a cost, and 12 is early.

What the Research Shows

The American Orthopaedic Society for Sports Medicine has published extensively on this. Youth pitchers who throw year-round without structured rest have significantly higher rates of UCL injuries (Tommy John territory) and shoulder impingement before the arm finishes developing — which is typically 15–17. The rotator cuff and UCL are still maturing at 12. Load them like an adult arm and you're rolling dice.

What Elite Programs Actually Do

  • Resist specialization before 14–15 — The feeder programs for high school and travel ball actively want kids who can do multiple things. A two-way player at 12 is a more attractive prospect at 15 than a specialist who burned out or got injured.
  • Evaluate the coach's motive — Is this about your son's development, or does the team need a pitcher right now? Those are different conversations. A good coach making a developmental call will explain the long-term reasoning. A coach solving a roster problem might not.
  • Pitch count limits matter more than position — The question isn't really "pitcher vs. hitter." It's "how many pitches per week, with how much rest?" Pitching with limits while still hitting is completely manageable.
The bottom line: A 12-year-old with a live arm who can also hit is a better high school prospect than a one-dimensional pitcher who peaked early. Keep both tools sharp. If he loves pitching and wants to commit at 15 or 16, that's the right and natural time for that conversation. Not now.

Ask the coach: "What does a pitch count and rest protocol look like if we go this direction? And can he keep his bat in the lineup?" The answer will tell you everything you need to know about whether this is the right coach to follow.

Video / Photography
"How do I make my videos look more cinematic without spending money on gear?"
Three moves that cost nothing: (1) Lock your frame — no handholding. Shaky footage looks amateur regardless of camera. (2) Shoot in the hour after sunrise or before sunset. Flat midday light kills footage. (3) Move slower than you think you need to — in editing, slow it down 20% more. That's where cinematic lives.
🎵
Music
"I want to start making beats but I have no idea where to actually begin."
One week, one tool: GarageBand is free and genuinely capable. Don't start with a full beat — start with a 4-bar loop with just a kick and snare. When that groove feels right, add bass. Add melody last. The mistake everyone makes is starting with too many elements and losing the rhythm foundation before it's solid.

Research
"Is it worth refinancing my mortgage right now or should I wait?"
Run the break-even math first. Take your monthly savings from the new rate and divide it into total closing costs. That's your break-even month. If you're moving or selling before that — don't refi. If rates drop more than 1% below yours and you're staying 5+ years — it's usually worth it. But the real question is: what do you plan to do with the monthly savings?

Sports Take
"What actually makes a good Little League coach? Not just stats, but the real stuff."
Three non-negotiables: (1) He talks to kids, not at them. (2) He remembers names fast and uses them. (3) He has a plan for the kid who's struggling, not just the star. Everything else is mechanics. The coach who wins championships but loses kids to the sport before high school is a bad coach. Full stop.
